Educomp Smart Privileged Classroom

The latest revolution in the sphere of education has been brought about by the advent of Educomp smart classes. These are a collection of interactive teaching modules that use visualizations, online quizzes, and motion graphics that are supposed to make school-based learning easier and less monotonous. As the above video showcases, this includes 2D and 3D content, covering K12 subjects that would help teachers to explain difficult and abstract concepts with clarity. Thus Educomp aims to mark a shift from the traditional blackboard-chalk approach to a multimedia-based, touch screen interactivity in the schooling system.

Sounds lucrative? All you need is a costly setup, half the blackboard space and a projection system in each classroom of the school and voila! You have transformed masterji‚Äôs Vidyalaya to a high tech smart class, with an exponential increase in response from the class, that wants to come to school every day.

Educomp is definitely a well-intended step towards personalising learning process and marks a technological change in the most primitive and conservative systems of India, its schools. It is a great example of a trickle-down effect of the rise of new media, that it has begun reaching out to every sphere of life imaginable.

But, on the other hand, Educomp is also the face of economic inequality and deprivation. This is because the basic warrant it stands upon is the availability, which is anything but universal for various reasons. These may include the inability to afford, lack of trained teachers, the informal setup that many schools run on, essentially facilities that only a fraction of the Indian schools are capable of ensuring to its pupils.

The effectiveness of the piece of media under question depends on the privilege of being able to go to an Educomp enabled school, which is selective. Most school-going children in India will never be able to know about the Educomp systems or the kind of learning it makes possible. Or even if they are told, they might not be able to interpret it due to lack of conditioning towards the assimilation of educational content, because of being underprivileged.

Quoting the company‚Äôs website itself:

Born in 2003, Educomp smart class has created shall we say, yet another divide of Have and Have Nots: schools that have Educomp smart class and schools that don‚Äôt.

Therefore we see two things: One, new media has expanded its audience to not just the tech-savvy teens or adults, but also creating a demand for new media-based resources amongst children who might just be learning to understand them for the first time. Second, the smart class system distinguishes its audience, on the basis of their privilege. And thus amongst the school-going children, whose privilege or lack thereof makes a difference by creating the rift of have‚Äôs and have-nots. This further reinforces in the minds of young impressionable children, a tangible and widened picture of the economic gap. Besides, another implication of a monopolized setup entering our children‚Äôs education is the threat to influence their soft minds. It is capable of forming new habits, inducing mental inactivity, easily subject to subtle propaganda and even interference from commercial models of content production, which can plant deep-rooted opinions in children.

To conclude, new media as a conveyance medium, is largely a beast whose power is still unmeasured. Thus one would like to ensure that it is unleashed upon their children in a regulated manner.
